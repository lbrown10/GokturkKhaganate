Nomadic Pastoralism

The usual lifestyle of a steppe nomad was spent herding livestock from place to place in order to find fresh pastures on which to graze. Usually this would be an irregular pattern of movement which would cause tribes to be often meeting new groups and sometimes move vast distances over the course of a lifetime. Generally food was the primary concern of a nomad, either from the livestock they travelled with or from hunting. The Silk Road
The Silk Road had been an established trade route for centuries spanning from the eastern Mediterranean to China. The Unification of the Central Asian Steppe however offered an opportunity for the Byzantines to shift the traditional trade routes north, through the G√∂kt√ºrk Khaganate, and thus avoid the Sassanid Empire entirely.

This trade route shift was greatly facilitated by the Sogdians, a group of people in northern Bactira. Having been an instrumental stop on the original Silk Road, the Sogdians had the trade connections and experience to shift trade north after they were incorporated into the G√∂kt√ºrk Khaganate. From West to East caravans carried glassware, horses, honey, fruits such as dates and grapes, and gold. Coming from China caravans carried tea, dyes, porcelain, spices and of course silk.

In the mid-6th century two Byzantine monks smuggled silkworms from China back to Byzantium with the help of the Turks. This would be the first time anywhere outside of the far east had the capacity to produce silk, a monopoly the Byzantines would keep for centuries.
